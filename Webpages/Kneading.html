How to Knead Your Bread!

Here are some guidelines on how to knead your loaf. Kind of like "How To Train Your Dragon" but less cool...

Please read them first and watch the video to further understand them. It isn't hard and more importantly, you will learn your own style that will suit you. My style might not suit you and that is fine but please do learn the concept.

  1. When you have mixed your ingredients together, you shall have a rough ball of dough. It might be sticky and that's okay.
  2. Add a little flour or oil to your surface. Flour will create a drier loaf but easier to clean up and helps prevent too much stickiness!
  3. Place your ball of dough onto the surface and pin it down with any hand. I use my left hand as I'm right-handed.
  4. Next push upwards using the ball of your spare hand. This will stretch the dough. Be sure not to stretch too far or you might tear the dough.
  5. Next I like to grab the stretched dough and fold it back itself. Do not worry about the dough sticking to your hands, it will start to become less sticky after more kneading.
  6. Turn the dough slightly clockwise or anticlockwise (choose one or the other) and repeat this technique for 5 to 10mins until the dough has a nice smooth skin.
  7. The more pressure you apply on the palm push, the quicker you will work your dough. This is also true with how quickly you repeat these steps.

And that is kneading, it's incredibly simple. The science is that you are strengthening the gluten strands by stretching them around without tearing them. However, if you do tear it, it is not an issue at all. You can just fold it back into the dough and continue.

How to tell if you have finished kneading?

The Smooth Skin

A kneaded dough will visably look different to an unkneaded dough. One of the key identifiers is the smooth skin that will be created from kneading. if we compare these two pictures:

We can easily see the second picture is different from the first and actually, that dough could have been further kneaded to further smooth the dough. One nice thing about hand kneading dough is that it is very hard to over-knead it, this is not true if you use a machine to knead your dough. Machines are far more powerful.

Give It A Poke

And if it springs back then we know that it is doing well. Here's a little video demonstrating what I mean.

The Windowpane Test

You have might of heard of this simple test. It is a quick way to see if how far you have gotten along with your kneading. It isn't completely conclusive, so don't use it to completely determine if you have finished your kneading. Do all 3 checks! But here is the idea.

  1. Remove a large piece of your kneaded dough, enough to stretch apart.
  2. Hold up the piece to a light source and block the light. An indoor light or a window will do.
  3. Take both sides of the piece and stretch it out slowly. If your dough has been well kneaded then it should stretch thin enough to see some light shine through.
  4. You should be able to do this without ripping the dough.

And that's it. It allows you quickly check after doing the other checks to tell if you have done a decent job of kneading. Now, onto how to shape your loaf! An extremely important step.

How to Shape Your Bread!

Shaping the dough is one of the most important steps. I would also argue more important than kneading/proving combined! Okay, that might be a slight exaggeration but it is definitely important to learn good techniques when possible. I'll be showing you both styles for free-form and for placing into a loaf tin. Moving on... Here is the technique for tinned loaves. I'll be assuming that your work surface is floured and the bread is properly kneaded and proved.

Shaping A Tinned Loaf

  1. Remove your proved dough and knock back the air out of it, which is folding it onto itself repeatly until it is similar in size before it was proved.
  2. Shape it into a rough circle by using the sides of both hands and tucking the sides in while rotating.
  3. When it is a circle, push down to flatten it and stretch it out to create an oblong rectangle.
  4. Fold the two far sides into the middle and push this down tightly.
  5. Take the longest edge closest to you and start to tightly roll it up like a swiss roll. This will create a seam/join running across on the base of your dough.
  6. Place this seam down into your oiled/buttered tin.

And that should give you nice tinned loaf. Make sure when you are rolling it, make it nice and tight. You'll get use to the practice when you attempt it often.

Shaping A Free-Form Loaf - The Bloomer

This is for a bloomer shaped loaf, something you might be familiar with if you ever gotten a "bakery" loaf from the supermarket. This is identical to shaping a tinned loaf. The key difference is when rolling it up. This time you want to be much softer.

  1. Remove your proved dough and knock back the air out of it, which is folding it onto itself repeatly until it is similar in size prior to proving.
  2. Shape it into a rough circle by using the sides of both hands and tucking the sides in while rotating.
  3. When it is a circle, push down to flatten it and stretch it out to create an oblong rectangle.
  4. Fold the two far sides into the middle and push this down.
  5. Take the longest edge closest to you and start to roll it up like a swiss roll. This will create a seam/join running across on the base of your dough.
  6. This seam will be base of your loaf. Place it seam down onto your baking tray.
